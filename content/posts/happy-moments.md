## happy-moments

For the longest time, I felt like I was living a life that belonged to someone
else. In my house, the word "career" was basically a synonym for **"Medicine."**
My parents had this vision of me in a white coat, a stethoscope around my neck,
carrying the title of Doctor. And because I love them, I tried to want it too.

But every time I sat down to study, my mind would wander to logic, loops, and
building things from scratch. I didn't want to study anatomy; I wanted to
understand architecture—the digital kind.

### The Two-Hour Talk

Today, I finally stopped pretending. I sat my parents down, and for about two
hours, we just... talked. Honestly.

It started with that familiar pressure—them pushing the stability of a medical
career and me feeling that knot in my stomach. But I realized their "forcing"
wasn't about control; it was about fear. They wanted me to be safe.

I had to show them that my safety lies in my passion. I explained that
**coding** isn't just a hobby for me; it’s how my brain works. I told
them about the rush of finally solving a bug after hours of searching,
and how that feeling is worth more to me than any prestige.

### The Promise

Eventually, the tension broke. They looked at me—really looked at me—and
said the words I’ve been waiting to hear:

> "Try your best. If this is where your heart is, go for it."

That was the happiest moment of my year. But it came with a heavy
responsibility. I didn't just win an argument; I made a commitment.
I promised them (and myself) that I wouldn't just "try"
programming—I would master it.

### What's Next?

The weight of the medical degree is gone. My desk is still the same, my
computer is the same, but everything feels different now. I’m no longer
a "future doctor" trying to hide a hobby.

**I am a programmer.** It’s time to prove that they were right to
trust me. Let’s get to work.
